\section{Electrical Power Management}

\begin{multicols}{2}
The realization of the 2026 50/50 power division mandates an electrical architecture capable of translating the massive 350 kilowatt (kW) potential of the Energy Storage System into precise, usable mechanical torque at the Motor Generator Unit-Kinetic (MGU-K) \cite{FIA_2026_PU_Regulations}. This section details the graduate-level power electronics, modulation theory, and state estimation algorithms required to safely and efficiently manage the 540 A Direct Current (DC) link within a high-frequency, severe thermal environment.

\subsection{Power Electronics Architecture: Inverter Design}

The inverter topology acts as the critical bridge between the 648 V DC pack and the 3-phase Permanent Magnet Synchronous Motor (PMSM). Given the extreme 350 kW load, Silicon Carbide (SiC) MOSFETs are universally selected over legacy Insulated-Gate Bipolar Transistors (IGBTs) \cite{sic_power_lit}. The SiC wide-bandgap architecture drastically minimizes switching losses at high pulse-width modulation (PWM) frequencies, offers superior high-temperature stability, and maintains lower $R_{ds(on)}$ conduction resistance, critical for surviving the 648 V DC link environment.

The selected topology is a standard 3-phase full-bridge inverter. With the peak DC current ($I_{dc}$) established at 540 A during maximum 350 kW deployment, the required phase Root Mean Square (RMS) current ($I_{phase(rms)}$) delivered to the MGU-K stators is derived as:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:phase_rms}
\begin{split}
I_{phase(rms)} &= \frac{I_{dc}}{\sqrt{3}} \\
&= \frac{540\,\mathrm{A}}{\sqrt{3}} \\
&= 311.77\,\mathrm{A} \approx 312\,\mathrm{A_{rms}}
\end{split}
\end{equation}

where $I_{dc}$ represents the peak draw from the battery pack.

The target switching frequency ($f_{sw}$) of the SiC MOSFETs is established at $\sim$20 kHz to balance acoustic noise, control loop bandwidth, and switching losses. The instantaneous switching power loss ($P_{sw}$) across the inverter bridge is approximated by:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:switching_loss}
P_{sw} = f_{sw} \cdot E_{sw} \cdot I_{peak}
\end{equation}

where $E_{sw}$ is the total switching energy (turn-on plus turn-off) per pulse, and $I_{peak}$ is the instantaneous peak phase current. A fundamental engineering tradeoff exists: while a higher $f_{sw}$ allows for smoother sinusoidal current synthesis (minimizing motor torque ripple), it linearly scales $P_{sw}$ to catastrophic levels when switching 540 A. Concurrently, the conduction losses ($P_{cond}$) generated during the "on" state of the SiC devices are:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:conduction_loss}
P_{cond} = I_{phase(rms)}^2 \cdot R_{ds(on)}
\end{equation}

where $R_{ds(on)}$ is the drain-to-source on-resistance of the SiC die. The summation of $P_{sw}$ and $P_{cond}$ across all six switch positions constitutes the total inverter thermal load. This massive thermodynamic influx necessitates a dedicated, liquid-cooled cold plate specifically sized to extract the combined $I^2R$ and switching heat flux without violating the $\sim$175$^\circ$C maximum junction temperature of the SiC chips.

\subsection{DC Link Capacitor Sizing}

A critically sized DC link capacitor ($C_{dc}$) is positioned immediately across the inverter's DC bus to absorb the violent current ripple generated by the high-frequency switching events, protecting the battery pack from high-frequency inductive voltage spikes. The required capacitance ($C$) is derived from the fundamental relationship governing voltage ripple ($dV$) over the switching period ($dt$):

\begin{equation} \label{eq:capacitance_deriv}
\begin{split}
C_{req} &= \frac{I_{dc\_ripple} \cdot dt}{dV} \\
&= \frac{540\,\mathrm{A} \cdot (50 \cdot 10^{-6}\,\mathrm{s})}{10\,\mathrm{V}} \\
&= 0.0027\,\mathrm{F} = 2.7\,\mathrm{mF}
\end{split}
\end{equation}

where $I_{dc\_ripple}$ is approximated by the 540 A peak phase draw, $dt$ is the inverse of the 20 kHz switching frequency ($\frac{1}{20,000\,\mathrm{Hz}}$ = 50 $\mu$s), and $dV$ is the strictly allowable voltage fluctuation target of 10 V to prevent inverter undervoltage faults. 

Applying an arduous motorsport safety factor of 2 to account for thermal degradation and extreme regenerative current transients, the final specified capacitance ($C_{dc}$) is 5.4 mF. Advanced film capacitor topology is unequivocally mandated over traditional electrolytic variants. Film capacitors provide drastically lower Equivalent Series Resistance (ESR) at 20 kHz, which is paramount for survival in the 131 kW thermal environment radiating from the battery chassis. The internal ESR power loss ($P_{esr}$) within the capacitor itself is:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:esr_loss}
P_{esr} = I_{dc\_ripple(rms)}^2 \cdot ESR
\end{equation}

where $I_{dc\_ripple(rms)}$ is the effective RMS ripple current. A high ESR would trigger rapid dielectric failure at 540 A peak draws.
\end{multicols}

\subsection{Field Oriented Control (FOC)}

Controlling the MGU-K at 15,000 RPM requires absolute decoupling of the torque-producing and flux-producing stator currents. This is achieved via Field Oriented Control (FOC) \cite{pmsm_foc_lit}. The algorithm begins by reading the instantaneous 3-phase currents ($i_a, i_b, i_c$) and reducing them using the Clarke Transformation to a 2-phase stationary orthogonal reference frame ($\alpha, \beta$):

\begin{equation} \label{eq:clarke_transform}
\begin{split}
i_\alpha &= \frac{2}{3} \left( i_a - \frac{1}{2}i_b - \frac{1}{2}i_c \right) \\
i_\beta &= \frac{2}{3} \left( 0 + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i_b - \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i_c \right)
\end{split}
\end{equation}

where $i_a + i_b + i_c = 0$ in a balanced wye-connected system. Subsequently, the Park Transformation rotates the stationary $\alpha\beta$ vectors by the measured rotor electrical angle ($\theta_e$) into a synchronous rotating reference frame ($d, q$):

\begin{equation} \label{eq:park_transform}
\begin{split}
i_d &= i_\alpha \cos(\theta_e) + i_\beta \sin(\theta_e) \\
i_q &= -i_\alpha \sin(\theta_e) + i_\beta \cos(\theta_e)
\end{split}
\end{equation}

By aligning the $d$-axis perfectly with the permanent magnet flux linkage ($\lambda_f$), the complex AC motor equations collapse into distinct, easily controllable DC quantities. The decoupled voltage equations in the synchronous $dq$ frame are:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:dq_voltage}
\begin{split}
V_d &= R_s i_d + L_d \frac{di_d}{dt} - \omega_e L_q i_q \\
V_q &= R_s i_q + L_q \frac{di_q}{dt} + \omega_e (L_d i_d + \lambda_f)
\end{split}
\end{equation}

where $R_s$ is stator resistance, $L_d$ and $L_q$ are the direct and quadrature inductances, and $\omega_e$ is the electrical rotational velocity.

Crucially, the electromagnetic torque ($T_e$) is now governed by:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:foc_torque}
T_e = \frac{3}{2} p [\lambda_f i_q + (L_d - L_q)i_d i_q]
\end{equation}

where $p$ represents pole pairs. The FOC algorithm allows Proportional-Integral (PI) controllers to independently regulate $V_d$ to control the flux field via $i_d$, and $V_q$ to dictate outright torque via $i_q$.

\subsection{Maximum Torque Per Ampere (MTPA)}

For a surface-mounted, non-salient PMSM architecture commonly utilized for extreme power density, the direct and quadrature inductances are virtually identical ($L_d \approx L_q$). This fundamental characteristic drastically simplifies the Maximum Torque Per Ampere (MTPA) strategy \cite{pmsm_foc_lit}. Because the reluctance torque component [$(L_d - L_q)i_d i_q$] collapses to zero, the optimal MTPA trajectory strictly enforces a $d$-axis current setpoint of zero:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:mtpa_id}
i_d^* = 0
\end{equation}

Consequently, all available stator current is directed exclusively to the $q$-axis to maximize torque output. Substituting $i_d = 0$ into Eq. \ref{eq:foc_torque} yields the simplified maximum torque relationship:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:max_torque}
T_{max} = \frac{3}{2} p \cdot \lambda_f \cdot I_{max}
\end{equation}

where $I_{max}$ represents the previously derived 312 A RMS limit limit. 

However, as the MGU-K accelerates toward its 15,000 RPM threshold, the induced back-EMF ($E = K_e \cdot \omega$) rapidly approaches the 648 V DC link voltage limit of the inverter. Once $\omega_e$ exceeds the base speed where back-EMF equals the terminal voltage, the motor enters the Field Weakening region. To continue accelerating, the FOC algorithm must actively drive $i_d$ negative. This negative $d$-axis current generates an opposing magnetic field that conceptually "weakens" the fixed permanent magnet flux ($\lambda_f$), thereby suppressing the back-EMF at the cost of reduced maximum $i_q$ capacity and lower torque. The requisite field weakening trajectory is defined continuously by observing the voltage limit ellipse.

\subsection{Space Vector PWM (SVPWM)}

Translating the PI controller $V_d, V_q$ reference voltages back into physical gating commands for the SiC MOSFETs relies on Space Vector Pulse Width Modulation (SVPWM). SVPWM is overwhelmingly preferred in motorsport over standard Sinusoidal PWM (SPWM) because it provides roughly 15\% higher utilization of the DC bus voltage, unlocking an absolute peak fundamental phase voltage of $V_{dc} / \sqrt{3}$ compared to $V_{dc} / 2$ for SPWM \cite{pmsm_foc_lit}.

The SVPWM algorithm synthesizes the target voltage reference vector ($V_{ref}$) by dynamically switching between eight fundamental states of the 3-phase bridge: six active non-zero voltage vectors ($V_1$ through $V_6$) forming a hexagon, and two zero vectors ($V_0, V_7$) located at the origin. 

The algorithm first identifies which of the six 60$^\circ$ sectors the rotating $V_{ref}$ currently occupies based on its angle $\theta$ relative to the $\alpha$-axis. The precise duty cycle timings required for the two adjacent active vectors ($T_1, T_2$) to geometrically synthesize $V_{ref}$ within the switching period ($T_s$) are derived as:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:svpwm_t1}
T_1 = T_s \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3} \cdot V_{ref}}{V_{dc}} \cdot \sin\left(\frac{\pi}{3} - \theta\right)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation} \label{eq:svpwm_t2}
T_2 = T_s \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3} \cdot V_{ref}}{V_{dc}} \cdot \sin(\theta)
\end{equation}

The remaining fraction of the switching period is populated by the zero vectors ($T_0$):

\begin{equation} \label{eq:svpwm_t0}
T_0 = T_s - (T_1 + T_2)
\end{equation}

The switching sequence is then meticulously arranged symmetrically (e.g., $V_0 \rightarrow V_1 \rightarrow V_2 \rightarrow V_7 \rightarrow V_2 \rightarrow V_1 \rightarrow V_0$) to minimize the absolute number of physical SiC switching transitions per cycle, drastically mitigating the formidable $P_{sw}$ thermal load defined in Eq. \ref{eq:switching_loss}.

\subsection{State of Charge Estimation}

Safely extracting laps from the exact 4 MJ energy limit requires highly rigorous State of Charge (SOC) tracking. The baseline measurement utilizes Ah-integration, or Coulomb counting:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:coulomb_counting}
SOC(t) = SOC(0) - \frac{1}{Q_{nom}} \int I(t)dt
\end{equation}

where $SOC(0)$ is the initial charge state, $Q_{nom}$ is the nominal pack capacity (1.714 Ah), and $I(t)$ is the dynamic discharge current. However, sensor noise and extreme 180 C current transients induce profound integration drift over a 50-lap race distance.

To continuously correct this drift, an Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) is implemented symbiotically with a Thevenin Equivalent Circuit Model (ECM) of the battery \cite{kalman_soc_lit}. The ECM estimates the terminal voltage ($V_{terminal}$) based on the Open Circuit Voltage (OCV) curve:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:ecm_voltage}
V_{terminal} = OCV(SOC) - I \cdot R_0 - V_{RC}
\end{equation}

where $R_0$ is immediate ohmic resistance, and $V_{RC}$ characterizes the voltage drop across parallel resistor-capacitor diffusion networks mapping polarization physics. The EKF observer compares this mathematically predicted $V_{terminal}$ against the physical sensor voltage measurement, actively updating the internal covariance matrices to correct the predicted $SOC(t)$ value in real-time, eliminating integration drift.

Crucially, to prevent rapid degradation of the NMC chemistry, the BMS firmware hard-codes a strict operational SOC window between 20\% and 95\%. Therefore, the true effective usable SOC spans only 75\% of the physical 1.11 kWh (4 MJ) pack structure. 

\begin{equation} \label{eq:usable_energy}
\begin{split}
E_{usable} &= (0.95 - 0.20) \cdot E_{pack} \\
&= 0.75 \cdot 1.11\,\mathrm{kWh} \\
&\approx 0.833\,\mathrm{kWh} \approx 3.0\,\mathrm{MJ}
\end{split}
\end{equation}

This severe 3.0 MJ usable constraint fundamentally redefines the lap deployment geometry.

\subsection{Energy Deployment Strategy}

Recalculating the macroscopic lap energy budget against the 3.0 MJ usable boundary reveals a drastic operational deficit. As derived previously, 5 zones of optimal 350 kW 4-second deployments yield a gross demand ($E_{demand}$) of 7.0 MJ per lap. While braking recovery nets 3.95 MJ, only 3.0 MJ can be safely utilized without violating the 20\% floor degradation threshold.

Therefore, the internal combustion engine (ICE) must operate aggressively in load-levelling mode, generating a net power surplus to supplement the staggering 4.0 MJ deficit:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:new_deficit}
\begin{split}
\Delta E_{lap(ICE)} &= E_{demand_{gross}} - E_{usable} \\
&= 7.0\,\mathrm{MJ} - 3.0\,\mathrm{MJ} \\
&= 4.0\,\mathrm{MJ}
\end{split}
\end{equation}

To govern this flow, the Central ECU executes a continuous trigraph state machine:
1. \textbf{HARVEST Mode}: Triggered under heavy braking. Kinetic energy flows backwards through the SiC inverter, regulated by the BMS to safely deposit the 3.95 MJ yield up to the 95\% ceiling.
2. \textbf{DEPLOY Mode}: Triggered on slow corner exits. The MGU-K acts as a 350 kW motor, aggressively discharging the 3.0 MJ usable budget to enforce the "torque fill" turbo-lag compensation strategy.
3. \textbf{CONSERVE Mode}: Triggered on long, high-speed straights. The MGU-K deployment is actively clipped to zero. The chassis switches mapping to X-mode (drag reduction), and the ICE takes full propulsive dominance while concurrently spinning the MGU-K as a light generator to directly combat the 4.0 MJ deficit.

\subsection{Protection and Isolation Systems}

Managing a 648 V architecture atop a violently vibrating carbon-fiber chassis requires unrelenting, fail-safe isolation per motorsport standards \cite{FIA_2026_Technical_Regulations}. Continuous Insulation Monitoring Devices (IMDs) inject high-frequency test signals to monitor chassis ground isolation. The minimum allowable isolation resistance ($R_{iso\_min}$) is derived to prevent lethal shock under worst-case leakage current ($I_{leakage\_max}$ typically < 1mA):

\begin{equation} \label{eq:isolation_res}
R_{iso\_min} = \frac{V_{dc}}{I_{leakage\_max}}
\end{equation}

Upon cold-start activation, the 5.4 mF DC link capacitor mimics a dead short. A dedicated pre-charge circuit utilizes a high-power resistor ($R_{precharge}$) to bypass the main HV contactors, limiting the devastating inrush current ($I_{inrush}$) to a safe 10A target:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:precharge_res}
R_{precharge} = \frac{V_{dc}}{I_{limit}} = \frac{648\,\mathrm{V}}{10\,\mathrm{A}} = 64.8\,\Omega
\end{equation}

Finally, catastrophic multi-tiered overcurrent thresholds trigger hardware gate-drive cutoffs at 1.2$\times$ peak nominal loads ($1.2 \cdot 540\,\mathrm{A} = 648\,\mathrm{A}$) within nanoseconds to arrest IGBT/SiC desaturation. Concurrently, software-level thermal cutoffs enforce a strict derating curve if continuous $I^2R$ cycling drives cell temperatures ($T_{cell}$) above the 60$^\circ$C threshold:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:thermal_derate}
P_{limit} = P_{max} \cdot \left[ 1 - k_{derate} (T_{cell} - 60^\circ\mathrm{C}) \right]
\end{equation}

where $k_{derate}$ dictates the aggressive slope of the power truncation.
\end{multicols}
